## Section Content Checklist

### Title (10-20 words)
- [ ] Addresses "what?", "why?", "how?"
- [ ] Uses content-predicting keywords
- [ ] No acronyms or jargon
- [ ] Reveals the main finding

### Abstract (<= 250 words, single paragraph)
- [ ] Motivation (1-2 sentences)
- [ ] Problem statement (1 sentence)
- [ ] Method summary
- [ ] Key results
- [ ] Implications
- [ ] No references, graphs, or acronyms

### Introduction (~500 words)
- [ ] General context (broad motivation)
- [ ] Specific research area and its importance
- [ ] Identification of gap/need/contradiction
- [ ] Specific research question
- [ ] Summary of approach
- [ ] Announcement of principal findings
- [ ] **Distinct from literature review** (avoid self-plagiarism)

### Methods (~1500 words)
- [ ] Materials, specimens, field sites, mathematical techniques
- [ ] Experimental setup and procedures
- [ ] Data analysis methods
- [ ] Sufficient detail for reproducibility
- [ ] Establishes credibility of approach
- [ ] Past tense, no first person
- [ ] Reference previously published procedures (no need to re-describe)

### Results (~2000 words)
- [ ] Present data directly supporting research question
- [ ] 4-5 display items (schematics, graphs, tables)
- [ ] Text and figures tell the same story independently
- [ ] Each figure/table self-contained with caption
- [ ] Highlight trends and comparisons
- [ ] **No interpretation** (save for Discussion)
- [ ] Written from reader's perspective, not chronological lab order
- [ ] Tables for precise numbers; Figures for trends

### Discussion (~1000 words)
- [ ] Interpret results to answer research question
- [ ] Limitations (keep brief)
- [ ] Relate results to previous literature
- [ ] Broader implications
- [ ] Specific future work directions
- [ ] Mirror structure of Introduction (narrow -> broad)

## Writing Style Principles (Strunk & White)
1. Choose a suitable structure and hold to it
2. Make the paragraph the unit of composition
3. Use the active voice
4. Use definite, specific, concrete language
5. Omit needless words
6. Avoid a succession of loose sentences
7. Express coordinate ideas in similar form
8. Keep related words together
9. In summaries, keep to one tense
10. Place emphatic words at the end of the sentence

**Key structural principle**: Hourglass shape - broad at start, narrow in middle, broad at end.

## Assessment Rubric (4 Criteria)

### 1. Background, Introduction and Scope (10%)

| Grade | Description |
|-------|------------|
| **Outstanding (>=85%)** | Authoritative command of research domain. Positions project at research frontier. Synthesises contemporary literature to identify clear research gap. Scope precisely defined with scholarly originality. |
| **Distinction (75-84%)** | Strong critical engagement with advanced literature. Project clearly situated in field. Scope well defined, but research gaps identified with limited depth/originality. |
| **Pass (60-74%)** | Adequate familiarity with field. Engagement largely summarative. Scope defined but weakly justified, minimal critical positioning. |
| **Unsatisfactory (<60%)** | Insufficient understanding. Sparse/outdated literature. Scope ill-defined or disconnected from current research. |

### 2. Understanding and Analysis (30%)

| Grade | Description |
|-------|------------|
| **Outstanding (>=85%)** | Exceptional methodological literacy and independent judgement. Methodology critically designed/adapted with discussion of theoretical assumptions, constraints, trade-offs. Analysis deeply analytical, reflexive, evaluative. Research-level reasoning. |
| **Distinction (75-84%)** | Advanced understanding of methodology. Clear justification of design choices. Limitations critically discussed (not fully problematised). Rigorous but less reflexive analysis. |
| **Pass (60-74%)** | Functional understanding of methods. Limited justification. Surface-level limitations treatment. Analysis largely descriptive/procedural. |
| **Unsatisfactory (<60%)** | Weak/flawed understanding. Critical limitations ignored. Analysis lacks rigour, coherence, or validity. |

### 3. Quality and Originality of Results (30%)

| Grade | Description |
|-------|------------|
| **Outstanding (>=85%)** | Technically robust, thoroughly validated, clear originality, potential publishability. Interpretation shows theoretical insight, critical comparison with existing work, explicit contribution articulation. |
| **Distinction (75-84%)** | Reliable, well-supported results meeting objectives. Elements of originality/advanced application. Well-reasoned interpretation, limited in theoretical depth. |
| **Pass (60-74%)** | Results meet objectives at basic level. Lack robustness/innovation/depth. Interpretation generally correct but under-developed. |
| **Unsatisfactory (<60%)** | Incomplete/unreliable/poorly validated. Interpretation incorrect or absent. No discernible contribution. |

### 4. Presentation (30%)

| Grade | Description |
|-------|------------|
| **Outstanding (>=85%)** | Publication-ready standard. Coherent, persuasive argumentation. Precise academic writing. Figures analytically informative, technically accurate, seamlessly integrated. Presentation enhances impact. |
| **Distinction (75-84%)** | Clearly structured, well written, strong academic tone. Figures appropriate and clear, some choices limit analytical effectiveness. |
| **Pass (60-74%)** | Adequately structured but lacks clarity/polish/argumentative coherence. Figures present but unclear, weakly explained, or inconsistently formatted. |
| **Unsatisfactory (<60%)** | Poorly organised, difficult to follow. Writing lacks academic clarity. Figures missing, misleading, or poorly integrated. |
